01 The Situation

Most product teams know something is wrong.
They can't prove what.

Support tickets rise. Onboarding numbers disappoint. NPS slips. But no single metric shows the whole picture — and standard analytics show you where users leave, never why.

"The root cause stays hidden under layers of accumulated design decisions no one can see from inside the product."

After 23 years and 16 global brands — from Intuit's small-business complexity to PayPal's trust breakdowns to Cisco's design system debt — I have developed a forensic eye for where products break and why. The pattern is consistent: teams optimise the metrics they measure, and go blind to the damage accumulating in the gaps between them.

A UX forensic audit surfaces those gaps. It doesn't just identify what's broken. It maps the shape of the problem, traces it to its organisational root, and delivers a business-cased roadmap with measurable success criteria.

Signs you may have hidden faultlines
  • Drop-off you can't explain. The funnel shows the cliff. Your team cannot agree on why.
  • Support tickets that keep recurring. The same confusion, different users, every week.
  • Features no one uses. Despite being core to your value proposition.
  • NPS comments that mention "confusing" without specifying what is confusing.
  • Onboarding that needs a call to complete. Your CS team is compensating for interface failure.
  • Churn you attribute to price when the real reason is trust erosion.
  • A design system that is inconsistent across teams in ways nobody planned.
  • Enterprise deals lost at demo. The product communicates complexity before value.

05 The Person

23 years building the
forensic eye.

What distinguishes a UX forensic auditor from a UX designer is not taste — it's diagnostic rigour developed across enough product failures to recognise the patterns before clients can name them.

I started my career building enterprise tools before "user experience" was a job title. What that meant in practice: I watched products accumulate debt from the inside — the stakeholder override that nobody documented, the legacy pattern nobody questioned, the metric nobody was watching. Twenty-three years of that leaves a residue. You start to see the shape of failure before you can name it.

The first time I noticed I was diagnosing rather than designing was at Waterloo Air in 2004. Engineers were taking 18 minutes to make a product selection decision the system was supposed to simplify. I didn't run a usability study. I watched how experts actually thought — what they reached for first, what they ignored — and rebuilt the information architecture around that sequence. Decision time dropped to 6 minutes. I didn't have a name for what I'd done. I just knew it wasn't design. It was closer to forensics.

That instinct compounded across IBM, Amazon, Cognizant, and the Fortune 500 engagements at Intuit, PayPal, and Cisco. The same pattern kept appearing: teams optimising the metrics they measured, going blind to damage accumulating in the gaps. By the time I was running the PayPal dispute resolution audit, I was doing the same five diagnostic moves every time — I just hadn't formalised them.

The Fault Topology Method™ was codified in 2024. It is not a retrospective label on 23 years of consulting. It is what those 23 years produced — a structured forensic process I am now offering as a standalone service for the first time. The psychological layer, drawing on Vervaeke's relevance realization, Friston's predictive processing, and Kahneman's prospect theory, was added during the formalisation — not to decorate the framework, but because it finally gave language to what I had been observing at a cognitive level across every engagement. The MA in Psychology (currently in progress) is the same project: not credentialism, but conceptual precision.

I am based in Bengaluru and work with global teams remotely. The methodology is built for asynchronous collaboration — most of the best diagnostic work happens in the data, not in the room.

07 Right Fit

Who this is for.

The FTM is built for product organisations where the cost of getting this wrong is measurable — and where a list of design opinions would not be enough.

Ideal client 01

Series B–D SaaS with a retention or activation problem

Your product works. Users are not adopting it the way you expected. The data shows where they leave but not why. You need a diagnosis, not a redesign.

Ideal client 02

Enterprise product leader with a design system debt crisis

Multiple teams. Multiple product surfaces. Inconsistency you can feel but cannot quantify. Velocity is slowing, but the cause is distributed across your organisation invisibly.

Ideal client 03

Fintech or healthcare CPO with a trust signal problem

Trust is your product. NPS is moving in the wrong direction. Support escalations are rising. Users cite "confusing" — but your interface is not obviously broken. This is a Trust Faultline.

This is probably not the right fit if

You need a redesign without a diagnosis first, an embedded designer for a long-term build, visual or brand design, or a research team to conduct multi-month studies. The FTM is a forensic audit service — its value is in finding and costing faultlines with high confidence, then handing a prioritised roadmap to your team to execute.
